 Artificial intelligence is frequently framed as a transformative research tool capable of accelerating scholarly workflows and expanding the scale of textual analysis. Yet such descriptions often reduce AI to a neutral instrument of efficiency, overlooking the deeper epistemic and cultural implications of integrating computational systems into the interpretive domains of the humanities.



This panel examines artificial intelligence through the methodological and critical frameworks of humanities scholarship. Disciplines such as history, archaeology, literature, and philosophy have long interrogated the processes through which knowledge is produced, mediated, and contested. These traditions provide an essential lens through which to analyse contemporary AI systems, whose outputs emerge not from objective neutrality but from historically situated datasets, linguistic hierarchies, and technical design choices.



Participants will explore how AI is beginning to reshape humanities research practices - from large-scale archival exploration to automated translation and synthetic text generation - while also raising fundamental questions about interpretation, expertise, and scholarly authority. Particular attention will be given to the ways in which algorithmic systems may reproduce historical biases embedded within digital corpora, and to the intellectual risks associated with treating computational fluency as a substitute for critical interpretation.



Rather than approaching AI as a purely technical development, the panel situates it within longer intellectual traditions concerned with language, representation, and the politics of knowledge. By foregrounding these perspectives, the discussion aims to move beyond both technological enthusiasm and disciplinary defensiveness toward a more nuanced understanding of how AI might reshape the humanities in the decades ahead.
