title: "Governing the AI 'Wail': Toward New Standards for Design Humility in Visual Knowledge Artefacts"
---

​As a DPhil researcher drawing on industry experience in public involvement, I have worked in a sector that actively rejects convoluted or over-claiming knowledge artefacts. When public trust is on the line, there is no margin for visuals that mask fragility behind slick design.

​Looking through this lens, we are navigating a dangerous epistemic step-change. As generative AI assumes the role of an "evolved printing press," it is mass-producing high-fidelity knowledge assets at scale. However, we lack the standards to govern this output. While the machine generates probabilistic synthesis, our graphical interfaces render definitive, hard-edged boundaries. By defaulting to the aesthetic legacy of frameworks like the Visual Notation for OWL Ontologies (VOWL)—built for seamless data representation—we rely on design conventions that inadvertently launder fragile algorithmic guesses into authoritative facts.

​This pursuit of a frictionless aesthetic has created the "Visual Wail": visual noise resonating with attention grabbing glare, glossing over the inherent hallucinations and messiness of probabilistic outputs. These interfaces perform an unearned sense of authority, fostering a quartet of epistemic failures: visual arrogance (Bravado), generic unengaging output (Dullness), data gaps (Omission), and incorrect underlying data (Error).

​This presentation argues that we must move beyond critique to develop a new Critical Visual Semantics Framework (CVSF). This framework serves as a provocation for a new standard of Visual Articulation. Just as speech requires the friction of consonants to turn a vocal sound into meaningful language, we must standardize visual "stops" and boundaries in AI-generated graphics.
