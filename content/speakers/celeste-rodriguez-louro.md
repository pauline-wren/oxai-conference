A/Prof. Celeste Rodríguez Louro is Associate Professor of Linguistics and Director of Language Lab at the University of Western Australia. She is also a Visiting Fellow at All Souls College, University of Oxford.


## Talk Abstract

In this talk, I revisit the increasingly common claim that generative AI is a “calculator for words”. While
calculators operate within narrow, rule-bound mathematical domains, large language models function within
far more complex social and linguistic environments. When we frame GenAI as neutral or inevitable, we risk
overlooking the cultural, political, and social choices embedded in its design and training data. I suggest that
moving beyond this analogy can open more thoughtful and productive conversations in universities.


My perspective begins from the understanding that social categories shape the technologies that increasingly
structure academic life. Datasets reflect assumptions about whose knowledge is visible, which languages are
represented, and what forms of communication are prioritized. Because of this, GenAI systems can mirror
existing patterns of inequality, including well-documented forms of algorithmic bias and the limited
representation of many of the world’s languages online. Recognizing these dynamics allows us to make more
informed decisions about how we integrate such tools into teaching and research.
I also reflect on the tendency to anthropomorphise AI. Although language models can appear responsive, they
do not possess subjectivity or intention. Their fluency can nevertheless encourage overreliance or “cognitive
offloading”. In academic contexts, this raises important questions about assessment, responsibility, and
agency.

Rather than rejecting GenAI or embracing it uncritically, I advocate for a human-centered approach grounded
in curiosity and reflection. By asking <u>what</u> we are automating, <u>why</u>, and <u>for whom</u>, we can work toward
integrating these technologies in ways that may enrich academic life.
